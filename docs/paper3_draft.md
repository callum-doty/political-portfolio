## Abstract

Paper I derives and estimates a static valuation model for campaign spending. Paper II operationalizes that valuation model sequentially, and in doing so discovers something the static model cannot express: capital held rather than spent has option value, because retaining it preserves the ability to react to information that has not yet arrived. Paper II names this gap, formalizes it as an unsolved Bellman equation, and shows its empirical cost — 65.5% of a live $393M 2026 budget recommended to non-competitive seats, roughly four months from Election Day, because nothing in the architecture assigns waiting any value at all.

It is tempting to read that gap as "solve the Bellman equation." This paper argues that framing is backward, in the same sense that "solve for the value of a stock option" is backward before anyone has specified how the stock price moves. An option's value is not an independent object to be derived; it is a corollary of a stochastic process for the underlying asset, $dS_t=\mu S_t\,dt+\sigma S_t\,dW_t$, plugged into a valuation equation whose functional form (Black–Scholes, or a Bellman recursion for American-style exercise) is standard machinery once that process is specified. The genuinely hard, genuinely novel step is specifying and defending the process — not solving the equation that consumes it.

This paper is the political-science analog of specifying $dS_t$. We define the campaign's full state vector $\mathbf X_t$ explicitly, decompose its evolution into a control component (fully derived already, in Paper I) and three stochastic or reactive components that are not derived anywhere in this project — the national environment process $G_t$, race-level idiosyncratic shocks $\varepsilon_{i,t}$, and opponent reaction $R_t(D_t)$ — and treat all three as co-equal objects of estimation, not as a primary process with two footnotes. Two of the three are now real, fitted quantities rather than research plans: opponent reaction is estimated from a real 2022/2024 panel (a tiered $\hat\eta(\text{tier})\approx0.25$–$0.48$ in genuinely contested races), and the national environment process is calibrated from a real, four-cycle historical generic-ballot series recovered this session, giving $\sigma_G(\Delta t)\approx0.18$–$0.20\times\sqrt{\Delta t}$ and showing that a random walk is a good approximation over the 3–9 month horizon that matters for $\Theta$ near Election Day. Race-level idiosyncratic uncertainty remains the hardest component: it is likely not estimable as a genuine time-varying process under the public-data-only constraint both prior papers inherit, and is instead treated as a bounded proxy borrowed from the national process's resolution rate. Only once $\mathbf X_t$'s transition law $P$ is specified does the value of waiting, $\Theta(t)$, cease to be an assumption and become a computable implication of $P$ — at which point solving for it is a standard application of regression-based Monte Carlo methods, not this paper's contribution. **This paper's contribution is $P$. $\Theta$ is what $P$ implies.**

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The question this paper answers, and the one it does not

Paper II asks how a committee should deploy capital sequentially, given a valuation model. In building that architecture, it discovers a gap: nothing in it prices the difference between "this race is worth funding" and "this race is worth funding *right now*." Paper II calls the missing object $\Theta$, states the Bellman equation it would need to solve, and stops there deliberately.

The natural next move is to treat that Bellman equation as the remaining problem and try to solve it. This paper argues that is the wrong next move, for a precise reason: a Bellman equation over an unspecified state-transition law is not merely difficult — it is not yet a well-posed mathematical object. Consider the equation itself,

$$V_t(\mathbf X_t,F_t) = \max_{0\le\mathbf p_t\le F_t}\ \mathbb E_P\!\left[\,V_{t+1}(\mathbf X_{t+1},F_t-\mathbf 1'\mathbf p_t+\text{fundraising}_t)\,\right]$$

Every symbol here is defined except one: $P$, the law governing how $\mathbf X_{t+1}$ is generated from $\mathbf X_t$. Without $P$, this expression has an expectation operator with nothing inside it to take an expectation *over*. It is not that solving this equation is hard without $P$ — it is that the equation does not yet say anything without $P$. Specifying $P$ is therefore not a preliminary step before the real work; it is the entire remaining scientific content of the problem. Once $P$ exists, computing $V_t$ (and therefore $\Theta$) is standard: simulate forward paths under $P$, and use a regression-based Monte Carlo method (Longstaff–Schwartz or a close relative) to estimate continuation values by backward induction. That machinery is decades old and well understood. It is not what makes this problem hard.

**This paper's job is to specify and, where the data allows, estimate $P$.** Everything about $\Theta$, reserve policy, and optimal stopping is a downstream implication of that specification, not a separate derivation.

### 1.2 The finance analogy, made precise

The correspondence to option pricing is exact enough to be load-bearing, not decorative:

| Finance | This project |
|---|---|
| Stock price $S_t$ | Campaign state $\mathbf X_t$ (defined in Section 2) |
| Price process $dS_t=\mu S_t dt+\sigma S_t dW_t$ | State-transition law $P$ (Sections 4–6) |
| Volatility $\sigma$ | $G_t$'s innovation variance, $\varepsilon_{i,t}$'s covariance structure, opponent reaction $R_t(D_t)$ |
| Option value $V(S_t,t)$ | Value function $V_t(\mathbf X_t,F_t)$ |
| Early-exercise value / American option premium | $\Theta(t)$, the value of not yet committing capital |
| Black–Scholes PDE / binomial tree / Longstaff–Schwartz | The Bellman recursion above, solved once $P$ is known |

No one asks "what is the value of waiting to exercise this option?" before specifying $\sigma$. The question is not answerable in that order. The same is true here, and Paper II's Bellman equation, exactly like an option-pricing PDE, is the *consumer* of a specified process, not a substitute for specifying one.

### 1.3 What motivates this now

Paper II's live 2026 run gives this urgency, not just theoretical motivation. At approximately four months from Election Day, the receding-horizon optimizer — which implicitly assumes $P$ contributes nothing beyond what the current period already knows — recommended 65.5% of $393M in deployable capital to Likely R and Safe R seats, including $5.6M to a district at PVI −17.5. This is the empirical size of the gap a specified $P$ would close.

## 2. The state vector $\mathbf X_t$

Paper II leaves $\mathbf X_t$ informal ("§3.1's state vector includes cash-on-hand, Cook rating, generic ballot... ") and its transition operator $f$ fully generic. Before any component of $P$ can be specified, $\mathbf X_t$ itself needs a precise definition — every later equation in this paper is a statement about how one piece of it evolves, and the Bellman equation in Section 1.1 is not rigorous until $\mathbf X_t$ is.

Grounding this in what Paper II's own code already implements (`dynamic/state.py`'s `CampaignState`/`RaceState`), the state at reporting period $t$ is

$$\mathbf X_t = \Big(\ \{\mu_{i,t},\ \sigma_{i,t},\ D_{i,t},\ R_{i,t},\ L_{i,t}\}_{i=1}^N,\ \ G_t,\ \ F_t\ \Big)$$

where, per race $i$: $\mu_{i,t}$ and $\sigma_{i,t}$ are the smoothed expected-margin and margin-uncertainty estimates (Paper I's valuation, re-estimated and EMA-smoothed each period per Paper II §3.3); $D_{i,t}$ and $R_{i,t}$ are cumulative Democratic- and Republican-aligned spending to date; $L_{i,t}$ is committed capital already irreversible for that race. At the aggregate level: $G_t$ is the national generic-ballot point estimate, and $F_t=B_t-\sum_iL_{i,t}$ is deployable capital (Paper II §3.2's ledger identity, already fully derived — nothing new here).

Two fields Paper II's `RaceState` dataclass reserves but leaves as unpopulated stubs — `cash_on_hand_d` and `cook_rating_t` — are omitted from $\mathbf X_t$ above because no process for them is proposed in this paper either; they remain explicitly out of scope, not silently assumed away.

**Each component of $\mathbf X_t$ evolves by exactly one of three mechanisms**, and naming this is what makes the Bellman formulation rigorous rather than notational:

| Component | Evolution mechanism | Status |
|---|---|---|
| $D_{i,t}$ | **Control**: $D_{i,t+1}=D_{i,t}+p_{i,t}$, the committee's own decision | Fully specified (it is the decision variable) |
| $\mu_{i,t}$ | **Control + two stochastic shocks**: $\mu_{i,t+1}=\mu_{i,t}+\Delta\mu_i(p_t)+\beta_i\Delta G_t+\varepsilon_{i,t}$ | Control term derived (Paper I); shocks require Sections 4–5 |
| $G_t$ | **Stochastic process**, exogenous to any single committee's decisions | Requires Section 5 |
| $R_{i,t}$ | **Reactive process**, a function of the *committee's own* control | Requires Section 6 |
| $F_t,\ L_{i,t}$ | **Deterministic bookkeeping** given $B_t$ and the ledger identity | Fully derived (Paper II §3.2) |
| $\sigma_{i,t}$ | Smoothed via Paper II's EMA; no independent process proposed here | Explicitly out of scope |

Of six rows, two are already fully solved by Papers I–II, one is definitional bookkeeping, and three are genuinely unresolved. This paper is about those three, and — departing from an earlier draft's imbalance — treats them as co-equal, not as a primary process ($G_t$) with two footnotes.

## 3. Why calibration precedes optimization

This deserves to be stated as its own claim, not buried in a limitations paragraph, because it is the paper's central methodological point: **without a calibrated $P$, the Bellman equation in Section 1.1 is not difficult to solve — it is undefined.** "Difficult" describes a well-posed problem that resists an easy method. An expectation operator with no specified distribution to integrate against is not that; it is a placeholder. Any number produced by "solving" it without a calibrated $P$ would be an artifact of whatever ad hoc distributional choice was smuggled in, not a result.

This has a direct methodological consequence for how this project should proceed: it would be a mistake to pick a convenient distribution for $G_t$ and $\varepsilon_{i,t}$ (a default Gaussian random walk, say) purely so that Section 1.1's equation can be exercised. That produces a number, but not a defensible one, and the number would carry false precision — exactly the failure mode Paper II's own Θ-free live run already illustrates in a different form (a model producing a confident-looking allocation from an under-specified objective). The correct sequencing is: estimate $P$ from data, honestly report where it cannot yet be estimated, and only then compute $\Theta$ — and where a component of $P$ cannot be estimated (Section 5 may be such a case), $\Theta$ should carry that uncertainty forward explicitly (e.g., as a bounded range) rather than resolving it by assumption.

## 4. Opponent reaction $R_t(D_t)$

*Elevated here to the first of three co-equal components — not, as in an earlier draft, an appendix to $G_t$ and $\varepsilon_{i,t}$. Operationally it may matter more than either: it does not just add noise to the state, it changes how much control the committee actually has.*

### 4.1 Why this is not "just another stochastic term"

The other two stochastic components ($G_t$, $\varepsilon_{i,t}$) enter $\mathbf X_t$'s transition additively, alongside the control term, without altering what the control term *means*. Opponent reaction is different in kind: if Republican-aligned spending responds to Democratic increments at some rate $\eta$, then the *effective* control is not $\Delta\mu_i(p_t)$ as Paper I derives it holding $R_i$ fixed — it is $\Delta\mu_i(p_t)$ evaluated against a moving $R_{i,t}$ that partially offsets the increment. `allocator.py` already encodes this mechanically (§8 of Paper I, reproduced in Paper II's optimizer calls):

$$R_i(D_i) = R_{i,\text{base}} + \eta\cdot\max(0,\ \text{party}_i - \text{party}_{i,\text{obs}}), \qquad \eta\in[0,1]$$

At $\eta=0$, Paper I's static gradient is exactly correct. At $\eta\to1$ (dollar-for-dollar matching), the log-ratio term that drives the entire MSG chain rule collapses toward zero — the committee's spending decision stops mechanically converting into margin movement at all. **This is not a refinement to the control term; it is a statement about how much of the control term is real.** A calibrated $\eta$ (or a richer reaction function $\eta(\cdot)$) is therefore not an optional addition to this paper's scope — it directly determines whether Paper I's chain-rule gradient, taken at face value in a multi-period setting, over- or under-states the committee's actual leverage.

A single scalar $\eta$ is very likely mis-specified: the economically plausible prior is that opponents match aggressively in Toss-Ups (where a marginal dollar changes the outcome) and largely ignore spending in races that are not competitive for either side. **The primary specification is therefore $\eta(\text{Cook tier})$, not a pooled scalar** — fit separately by competitiveness tier from the start, with a single pooled $\eta$ reported only as a secondary, illustrative average. Fitting the scalar version first and tiering "later" would risk publishing a leverage estimate that is systematically wrong in exactly the races (Toss-Up, Lean) that matter most for the optimizer's marginal decisions.

### 4.2 Current treatment, and a real data-quality obstacle found this session

$\eta$ is currently a hand-set scenario parameter (0, 0.5, or 1.0), chosen to bound a range of assumed opponent behavior, not estimated. The infrastructure to estimate it exists in this repository — `dynamic/simulate.py`'s one-step-ahead historical harness and `fetch_live_ies.py`'s dated FEC Schedule E parsing together mean $(D_{i,t}, R_{i,t})$ can be reconstructed at multiple points within the 2022 and 2024 cycles from data already on disk — but the raw data has a real, checked problem that must be fixed before that reconstruction is trustworthy.

We checked the specific concern that historical Schedule E is poorly attributed to districts pre-2018 (the a priori worry going in). **That specific concern does not hold in this repository's data**: `can_office_dis` (district attribution) is blank in only 0.1–0.3% of House-general IE rows in *every* cycle from 2012 through 2024 — pre-2018 is no worse than recent cycles on this dimension, because every IE row is filed against a specific candidate ID, not a generic national bucket. What we found instead, checking `exp_date` (needed for the date-bucketed reconstruction specifically):

| Cycle | n (House, general) | blank `exp_date` | blank `can_office_dis` |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2012 | 12,835 | 0.0% | 0.2% |
| 2014 | 8,012 | 12.9% | 0.1% |
| 2016 | 5,963 | 17.5% | 0.3% |
| 2018 | 24,073 | 11.7% | 0.1% |
| 2022 | 19,401 | **33.3%** | 0.1% |
| 2024 | 19,881 | **28.4%** | 0.1% |

Missing dates are worse in the *recent* cycles this paper actually needs, not the older ones — the opposite of the a priori concern. Separately, and more seriously: 2022's raw file contains one corrupted row (`Akhlaghy, Nader` / `COMMITTEE 300`, `exp_amo=$10,000,000,000`, with `agg_amo=2024.00` — a value that looks like a year leaked into the wrong field, i.e. a parsing bug, not real spending), duplicated twice, and more broadly **26% of all 2022 IE rows (5,097/19,401) share a duplicated `tran_id`**. We confirmed this does *not* contaminate any number already reported in Papers I/II — the affected district (NY-05)'s actual `d_total`/`r_total` in `race_table_baseline_2022.csv` are sane ($1.76M/$126K), and the table's maximum value anywhere is $29M, so the consolidated pipeline Papers I/II draw from already filters or dedupes this upstream of what gets reported. It is, however, a live blocker for this section's raw, transaction-level reconstruction specifically, which has not been built yet. **A `tran_id`-deduplication and implausible-`exp_amo` filtering step is a prerequisite to §4.3, not an optional data-cleaning nicety.**

### 4.3 Proposed specification

$$\Delta R_{i,t}^{\text{IE}} = \eta(\text{tier}_i)\cdot\Delta D_{i,t-1}^{\text{IE}} + u_{i,t}$$

with race fixed effects applied via within-(cycle, district) demeaning (not dummy columns — pooling ~800 district-cycle observations across biweekly periods makes a dummy matrix wasteful), pooled across the reconstructed and de-duplicated 2022/2024 panel, $\eta$ interacted with Cook tier.

## 5. The national environment process $G_t$

### 5.1 Candidate specifications

- **Random walk**: $\Delta G_t\sim N(0,\sigma_G^2\Delta t)$ — variance grows unboundedly with horizon.
- **Mean-reverting (Ornstein–Uhlenbeck)**: $dG_t=\kappa(\bar G-G_t)dt+\sigma_G\,dW_t$ — variance saturates.

These have materially different implications for $\Theta$ in principle: a random walk implies option value keeps growing the longer one waits; mean reversion implies most resolvable uncertainty is realized within a bounded window. In practice, this distinction should not be over-indexed on. The generic ballot is bounded by underlying partisanship, so AIC will very likely favor OU over a random walk almost regardless of the data — but the mean-reversion speed $\kappa$ implied by a 12-month lookback is typically slow enough that over the 3–6 month horizon actually relevant to $\Theta$ near Election Day (Paper II §7.1's live run is ~4 months out), RW and OU are nearly indistinguishable in their implications. Formal model selection between them is a secondary exercise, not the primary deliverable of this section.

**The primary deliverable is instead the empirical, non-parametric term structure $\sigma_G(\Delta t)$ itself** — realized volatility as a direct function of horizon, estimated straight from the historical series without forcing a Gaussian process form onto it first. §5.3 now reports this pooled across four real historical cycles plus the live 2026 series, rather than the single-path exercise an earlier draft was limited to.

### 5.2 Data requirement — resolved this session

This required a **historical, dated, multi-cycle** generic-ballot series (2018, 2020, 2022, 2024), comparable to the live 2026 feed this project already ingests. It did not exist in this repository as of the previous draft — `data/raw/rcp/` (RealClearPolitics) is an empty placeholder and blocked by bot protection when checked directly (`data_catalog.md` §1.10), and `generic_ballot_by_cycle.csv` has exactly one static value per cycle, no dates at all — but a real source was found and is now in this repository: FiveThirtyEight's own live generic-ballot data endpoint, discontinued after ABC News shut down 538 in 2023, recovered via a Wayback Machine snapshot (`data/raw/generic_ballot/generic_ballot_historical_538.csv`; provenance in `data_catalog.md` §1.8b). This snapshot retained the full cumulative daily trend estimate for all four cycles: 1,142–1,174 daily rows each, 2017-04 through 2024-11.

### 5.3 Term structure — estimated on real, multi-cycle data

`scripts/estimate_gb_volatility.py` computes realized volatility of $\Delta G$ pooled across all four historical cycles plus the live 2026 series, at the same horizons as the original single-cycle exercise:

|---|---|---|---|---|
| 30 days | 5 | 1.09 | 0.199 | 0.186 |
| 60 days | 5 | 1.54 | 0.199 | 0.186 |
| 90 days | 5 | 1.89 | 0.199 | 0.183 |
| 180 days | 5 | 2.70 | 0.201 | 0.186 |
| 270 days | 5 | 3.29 | 0.200 | 0.178 |
| 365 days | 5 | 3.81 | 0.199 | 0.160 |
| 450 days | 5 | 4.17 | 0.197 | 0.128 |

This changes the qualitative conclusion from the single-cycle exercise, not just its precision. Pooled across genuinely independent cycles, std/√days is **remarkably stable from 30 through 270 days** (0.183–0.201) rather than steadily declining — the apparent mean-reversion signal in the original one-path exercise was a small-sample artifact, not a real feature of the process. A visible decline only emerges past ~365 days (historical-only: 0.160 at 365 days, 0.128 at 450 days), consistent with §5.1's prediction: **a random walk is a good approximation over the 3–9 month range that actually matters for $\Theta$ near Election Day, and the RW/OU distinction only starts to matter at horizons beyond about a year** — this project's live 2026 application is ~4 months out, squarely inside the range where the two specifications barely differ.

**Working number for the calibration**: $\sigma_G(\Delta t)\approx0.18$–$0.20\times\sqrt{\Delta t\text{ (days)}}$, historical-cycles-only preferred for methodological cleanliness (§1.8b's caveat: 538's own aggregation vs. this project's raw-poll smoothing are not identically constructed). At the ~120-day horizon relevant to Paper II's live run, this gives $\sigma_G\approx2.0$ points — a real, cross-cycle-supported number, not a single-path guess. `outputs/gb_volatility_term_structure.csv` and `outputs/gb_volatility_term_structure_historical_only.csv` are the saved results; `outputs/gb_volatility_by_cycle.csv` breaks it down cycle by cycle for inspection.

### 5.4 Validation standard for the assembled simulator

"Does the simulator produce trajectories that look like real ones" is too low a bar once $\hat\eta$, $\hat P_G$, and Section 6's treatment are assembled into the full state-transition simulator of Section 2. The validation metric this project should hold itself to is **rank correlation between simulated $\mu_{i,t}$ and eventual realized margin**, checked at a fixed pre-election horizon (e.g., simulated-as-of-September paths vs. November outcomes, using 2022/2024 as held-out validation cycles). A simulator that cannot rank-order competitive races correctly by that point produces a $\Theta$ that is garbage-in-garbage-out regardless of how carefully Sections 4–6 were individually calibrated — this check belongs before Section 7's Bellman step, not after.

## 6. Race-level idiosyncratic uncertainty $\varepsilon_{i,t}$

### 6.1 What already exists, and what's missing

Paper I's $\sigma_i$ model is a **cross-sectional** fit — how much residual uncertainty a race of a given type carries once, not how that uncertainty evolves or resolves over a cycle. Paper I's factor covariance is similarly **static**. A genuine $\varepsilon_{i,t}$ process requires knowing how much of a race's idiosyncratic uncertainty resolves per unit time, and how that resolution correlates across races beyond what $G_t$ already captures (a primary result, a retirement, a redistricting ruling).

### 6.2 Data constraint — likely permanent, and why the obvious fallback is dangerous

This requires **district-level polling history** across the competitive universe. In practice this is worse than "sparse": in a typical recent cycle, a small minority of House districts — well under 10% of the competitive universe — receive two or more public polls at all, and most of those cluster in the final weeks before Election Day. There is not enough data to fit a genuine time-series process for $\varepsilon_{i,t}$ per race; for the large majority of races, the effective sample size for "how does this race's idiosyncratic uncertainty resolve over time" is one observation or zero.

*Re-checked 2026-07-20 against reviewer feedback on this paper's missing-data catalog (`docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §12): VoteHub's polling API (already this project's source for live generic-ballot polls, §5.2) carries a `us-representative` poll type with real per-district subjects. Checked directly — 28 districts have any live 2026 coverage, and critically, no historical (2022/2024) district-level panel exists at this endpoint. This confirms rather than reopens the conclusion above: the new data is real and is now wired into `dynamic/state.py`'s `RaceState` as a live monitoring field (`poll_mean_t`/`poll_sigma_t`/`poll_n_t`/`poll_trend_t`, never fed into $\mu_i$, same treatment as the existing generic-ballot-polls precedent), but it cannot retroactively supply the historical panel this section's $\varepsilon_{i,t}$ estimation problem needs. The data gap this section describes is unchanged; what changed is that the live 2026 application now has a real (if sparse) polling signal it did not have before.*

An earlier version of this plan proposed falling back on Paper I's static, cross-sectional $\sigma_i$ as a stand-in for $\varepsilon_{i,t}$'s distribution. **That fallback is not a neutral simplification — it is wrong in a specific, consequential direction.** Treating $\varepsilon_{i,t}$ as a static draw from $\sigma_i$'s cross-sectional distribution implicitly assumes all of a race's idiosyncratic uncertainty resolves instantaneously, which is equivalent to assuming there is no idiosyncratic information left to arrive — exactly backward, since it would make $\Theta$ collapse toward zero for the wrong reason (nothing left to wait for) rather than the right one (nothing left to *learn*).

**Proposed fix**: borrow the resolution *rate*, not the distribution, from Section 5's calibrated national process, and apply it as a shrinkage/decay factor on the static cross-sectional $\sigma_i$:

$$\sigma_{i,t} = \sigma_i^{\text{static}}\cdot\sqrt{1-e^{-\lambda(T-t)}}$$

where $\lambda=0.00536\,\text{day}^{-1}$ ($\tau=186.5$ days) is fit from $\sigma_G(\Delta t)$'s term structure (§5.5) rather than estimated separately per race — the assumption being that idiosyncratic information resolves at a rate comparable to national information, absent any race-specific data to say otherwise. This is explicitly a proxy, not a fitted $\varepsilon_{i,t}$ process, and should be reported as one: it prevents $\Theta$ from being inflated by an implausible "surprise arrives all at once on Election Day" assumption, but it does not constitute having estimated race-level dynamics from data, because the data to do so does not exist in usable quantity. If, after checking, district-level polling density turns out to be higher than assumed here, this section's conclusion should be revisited rather than assumed.

## 7. From a calibrated $P$ to $\Theta$ — the easy part

### 7.0 A necessary condition, checked before asking the value-of-waiting question

Before asking whether *preserving* the ability to reallocate capital has positive expected value — the question the rest of this section exists to answer — a logically prior one has to be settled: does new information even change what an optimal allocator wants to do? A necessary condition for dynamic optimization is that the optimal allocation changes as information arrives. This was tested directly (`docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §11, added 2026-07-20), not assumed: re-solving the nonlinear optimizer (§8.2's diminishing-returns-respecting allocator, not the LP) against real historical state snapshots from the 2022 and 2024 held-out cycles, holding the total DCCC party budget fixed and identical at each snapshot so that only the *information* available differs. Across both cycles, roughly one-third to one-half of the top twenty targeted races change between a 60-day-out and a 14-day-out snapshot (Jaccard overlap 0.54 and 0.67, respectively) — real, substantial turnover, not a rounding effect. Thus, even before considering the value of sequential decision-making, the allocation policy itself is demonstrably state-dependent: adaptive reallocation is a meaningful decision problem in this domain, not a theoretical abstraction being modeled for its own sake.

Having shown that the optimal policy evolves with information, this section now asks the complementary question: whether *preserving the ability to adapt* — rather than committing to a single allocation now — has positive expected value. The two findings are not in tension. §7.0 establishes that the underlying decision problem is genuinely dynamic; §7.2/§8.2 characterize the optimal policy within the assumptions of this paper's specified $P$, and find that immediate deployment wins there despite the targeting turnover just demonstrated — a real, honestly-scoped finding about *this* $P$, not evidence that the dynamic framing was unnecessary. Full detail, including the specific races that swap in and out of the top twenty, is in `docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §11.

Once Sections 4–6 (or as many as prove tractable) specify $P$, computing $V_t(\mathbf X_t,F_t)$ and therefore $\Theta(t)=V_t^{\text{wait}}-V_t^{\text{deploy-now}}$ is standard: simulate forward paths of $\mathbf X_t$ under $P$; at each step, regress simulated continuation values on a basis of current-state features (a Longstaff–Schwartz-style approach); proceed by backward induction from $T$. This is well-established machinery. **We say this explicitly so that it is not mistaken for this paper's contribution.** The contribution is Sections 4–6; this section is what those sections are *for*, not a fourth research question alongside them.

### 7.1 Simulator self-consistency check (the gate before Longstaff–Schwartz)

A multi-period simulator built from Sections 4–6's calibrated components is not automatically trustworthy just because each component was individually validated — per-step implementation errors (an incremental-variance decomposition that doesn't telescope correctly, a sign error in how $\eta$ feeds $R_{i,t}$, a discretization artifact from the period grid) can compound silently across many steps and only surface in aggregate statistics. This project already has one demonstrated instance of code that looked correct but didn't match its own specification (the MSG gradient bug, Paper I) — the same discipline applies here before any simulated path is trusted for pricing.

**Deriving the incremental $\varepsilon_{i,t}$ process.** Section 6.2 gives the *cumulative* remaining-uncertainty target, $V_i(t)=\sigma_i^2(1-e^{-\lambda(T-t)})$. For a period grid $n=0,\dots,N$ (period length $\Delta$, $N$ = Election Day), the per-step increment variance is $v_{i,n}=V_i(n)-V_i(n+1)$, and drawing $\varepsilon_{i,n+1}\sim N(0,v_{i,n})$ independently at each step reproduces the cumulative schedule exactly by telescoping ($\sum_{n=t}^{N-1}v_{i,n}=V_i(t)$, with $V_i(N)=0$ matching $\Theta(T)=0$). This is an independent-increment process matched to a prescribed, shrinking variance schedule — not a Brownian bridge in the strict sense, since nothing here conditions on a known terminal value.

**Design of the check.** `scripts/simulate_and_validate.py` simulates $G_t$ (standalone random walk at $\sigma_G(\Delta t)$; *not* fed into $\mu_i$, per §5.5's scope boundary), $R_{i,t}$ (driven by the **real** historical $\Delta D_{i,t}$ path plus calibrated $\hat\eta(\text{tier})$ plus residual noise drawn from the actual fitted regression residuals), and $\varepsilon_{i,t}$ (the incremental decomposition above) — holding the control $D_{i,t}$ at its real historical trajectory throughout, since this checks $P$'s mechanics, not a spending policy. 5,000 paths per cycle (2022, 2024):

| Check | 2022 | 2024 | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| A: simulated $\sigma_G(\Delta t)$ / target, at 28/56/112 days | 1.001 / 1.002 / 0.998 | 0.996 / 0.993 / 0.990 | Matches |
| B: $\hat\eta$ recovered from simulated paths vs. input (single-path recovery) | 0.344 vs. 0.321 | 0.159 vs. 0.175 | Matches — no sign/scale bug |
| C: cumulative $\varepsilon$ variance vs. target, mean/max relative error | 1.5% / 3.7% | 1.3% / 4.2% | Matches (residual is Monte Carlo noise at 5,000 paths) |
| D: simulated margin spread / target remaining-uncertainty | 0.98–1.02 | 0.98–1.02 | Matches |

All four pass. **This confirms the simulator faithfully implements what Sections 4–6 calibrated — it does not re-validate the calibration itself.** §5.5's caveats (†$\hat\eta$'s magnitude, not sign, unstable across a leave-one-cycle-out refit; $\sigma_G$'s possible late-cycle directional bias) are real properties of $P$ as calibrated, and this check confirms they now propagate faithfully into the simulator rather than being compounded by a new implementation error. `outputs/simulator_self_consistency.json` is the saved output.

### 7.2 The one implementation detail that is not optional: compress the regression basis

"Standard machinery" still has a specific failure mode here that must be designed around from the start, not discovered after a slow, overfit first attempt. Section 2's state vector carries $\{\mu_{i,t},\sigma_{i,t},D_{i,t},R_{i,t},L_{i,t}\}$ *per race* — on the order of 400+ races. Regressing simulated continuation values on a per-race feature basis (raw or polynomial) is not merely slow, it is a data-analytic mistake at the sample sizes involved: with $K\sim10^4$ simulated paths and $\sim$15 time steps, the regression has on the order of $10^5$ observations against a feature matrix with hundreds of columns — invertible in principle, but the resulting fit would overfit badly and say more about simulation noise than about $\Theta$.

**The regression basis must be a small set of portfolio-level aggregate features, not per-race features.** A minimal basis of four to six moments is standard practice for exactly this class of problem (basket-option pricing, where the same dimensionality problem arises from pricing an option on many underlyings at once):

1. $\mathbb E[\text{Seats}]_t=\sum_i\Phi(\mu_{i,t}/\sigma_{i,t})$ — the portfolio's current level.
2. $\text{Var}[\text{Seats}]_t=\mathbf s_t'\Sigma\mathbf s_t$ — the portfolio's current risk (Paper I's factor covariance, already available).
3. $\max_i\text{MSG}_{i,t}$ — the value of the single best marginal dollar available right now.
4. A near-threshold count: the number of races within some small margin (e.g., 2 points) of the majority-determining threshold — a direct proxy for how much of the portfolio's outcome is still genuinely undecided.

Regressing $V_{t+1}$ on this basis (with squares/cross-terms if needed) keeps the Longstaff–Schwartz step fast and well-conditioned, and is the specification this paper commits to rather than a per-race alternative that would not run at this state-space size in practice.

### 8.1 The closing deliverable and its pre-registered success criteria

The empirical payoff of this entire research line is a single comparison: re-run Paper II's live 2026 scenario with $\Theta(t)$ folded into the receding-horizon objective (as a reserve fraction or shadow price on $F_t$; no other change to Paper II's architecture), and compare the resulting Safe R/Likely R allocation share against the 65.5% baseline (Paper II §7.1). We commit to the following interpretation **before** running it, so the result cannot be read post hoc into whatever number comes out:

- **Result < 15%**: the calibration is doing real work — a $\Theta$-aware optimizer concentrates capital sharply relative to the greedy baseline, consistent with the prediction that low-delta, low-volatility safe seats have little to gain from patience while Toss-Ups do.
- **Result 15–30%**: partial effect — directionally consistent with $\Theta$ mattering, but the calibrated $\hat\eta$ or $\hat\sigma_G$ is likely too weak to fully price the option value at stake.
- **Result > 30%**: the $\Theta$ penalty as calibrated is too weak to matter, and the specific reason (undersized $\hat\sigma_G$, undersized $\hat\eta$, or an inadequate $\varepsilon_{i,t}$ proxy) should be diagnosed and reported, not smoothed over.

**This comparison should be published regardless of which band it falls in.** A result showing the greedy solve was already close to optimal — i.e., that waiting does not matter much in practice — is a legitimate, publishable finding about this specific decision problem, not a failed calibration to be hidden. The only unpublishable outcome is not running the comparison at all.

### 8.2 The comparison, run: result and a methodological lesson

`scripts/solve_bellman_lsm.py` implements the backward induction: 2026 live universe, biweekly periods from today to Election Day, $K=2000$ simulated paths, the compressed four-feature basis (§7.2), and the closed-form "deploy now, let remaining drift resolve" value derived above. Two runs were made, one using residual-noise scales fit on 2022, one on 2024, per §5.5's cycle-instability caveat.

**A correction found after this section was first drafted, while preparing the natural next follow-up document (`docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §0):** checking `solve_bellman_lsm.py` directly showed that $\hat\eta$ itself was never used inside `run_lsm()` — it was passed in and stored for the output record, but never multiplied into anything. This was not incidental: under the "wait" branch, discretionary $D_{i,t}$ never changes, so $\eta\cdot\Delta D_{i,t}=\eta\cdot 0=0$ regardless of $\hat\eta$'s value. The original two runs therefore differed only in `resid_std_by_tier` (opponent spending variation *unexplained* by reaction), not in $\eta$ — "two $\eta$-brackets" was an inaccurate description of what was actually run. Separately, $G_t$ was never simulated in this script at all (no `g_paths`, no reference to §5's calibrated $\sigma_G$), consistent with §5.5's scope boundary but meaning the national-environment calibration was completely absent from that run, not merely present-and-symmetric.

**Both gaps have now been closed, and the run redone** (`docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §0, implementation log §4). $\hat\eta(\text{tier})$ now discounts the deploy branch's linearized MSG gradient — the entire deploy-branch increment is new spend, against which $R_{i,t}$ reacts at rate $\eta$, matching `allocator.py`'s `_msg_vec` adjustment ($c\cdot(1/D-(1+\eta)/T)$ in place of $c\cdot(1/D-1/T)$). $G_t$ is now simulated in the wait branch as a standalone zero-drift random walk at $\sigma_G(\Delta t)$ and added as a fifth continuation-value regression feature — never fed into $\mu_i$'s structural formula, per §5.5's boundary. A third gap (giving $\eta$ something to react to *on the wait branch*, via candidate-committee floor growth) was found to be **not implementable from real data**: `candidate_disbursements_{cycle}.csv` contains only a single cycle-cumulative-final total (`TTL_DISB`) with no per-filing date field anywhere in this repository — the identical, already-documented gap in `dynamic/ledger.py`'s `RealizedSpendCommitmentSource` docstring for coordinated expenditures. $D_{i,t}$ therefore remains genuinely fixed on the wait branch, for a real and likely-permanent data reason, not an oversight; this is reported rather than papered over with an invented pacing curve. A separate check (`scripts/estimate_gb_ou_drift.py`) fit an OU-with-drift model on the pooled four-cycle series and found the drift term statistically indistinguishable from zero ($p=0.37$) and numerically negligible at the live decision's ~110-day horizon, confirming a zero-drift walk is the right specification for $G_t$ here.

**$\Theta(t)$ is negative (deploy favored) at every period in the 2022-residual-scale run**, ranging $-0.13$ to $-0.52$ expected seats, most strongly at $t=0$ (today, $-0.517$) and again near the terminal boundary. The 2024-residual-scale run now shows a **wider and somewhat stronger positive-patience window** than the pre-correction run — $\Theta>0$ for five consecutive periods (84 down to 28 days out: $+0.047,+0.097,+0.123,+0.076,+0.016$), peaking at 56 days out — before turning negative again at 14 days out ($-0.133$) and at $t=0$ ($-0.039$). The fifth regression feature, $G_t$, behaved exactly as predicted: its fitted coefficient is small and statistically unremarkable at every period ($|\hat\beta_{G}|<0.02$, $p\in[0.18,0.83]$) — a correctly-wired but currently-null feature, consistent with $G_t$ never entering $\mu_i$ and the drift check above finding nothing for it to pick up.

At $t=0$ — the one decision that actually matters for a live recommendation — **both runs still recommend deploying the entire $\$393$M reserve immediately** ($\Theta(0)=-0.517$ and $-0.039$ respectively; both report `frac_deploy_now=1.0`, though at $t=0$ every simulated path shares an identical pre-shock state, so this reflects one decision, not 2,000 independent ones). **The correction changed *how* this result was obtained — and moved the 2024 bracket's $\Theta(0)$ much closer to zero, with a real, somewhat larger mid-cycle patience premium than the incomplete model showed — but it did not reverse the top-line recommendation.** Full implementation and re-run details are in `docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §4. *(These two $\Theta(0)$ figures are superseded by §8.5's `beta1_open` correction: $-0.633$ and $-0.109$ respectively. The recommendation is unchanged — both still deploy unanimously — and the correction moves both further from indifference, not closer.)*

**A third scenario, added 2026-07-17 (`docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §6): $\hat\eta$ drawn per simulated path from the empirical 7-cycle bootstrap distribution, replacing two hand-picked brackets with a real, quantified parameter-uncertainty propagation.** Each of the 2,000 paths draws its own tier-specific $(\hat\eta, \text{resid\_std})$ pair from a randomly chosen historical cycle (2012–2024), held fixed for that path's whole campaign — the "random effects, draw once per simulated election" design motivated by the confirmed finding that $\hat\eta$ varies significantly cycle-to-cycle for 5 of 7 tiers (§5.5) but shows no evidence of *within*-cycle drift (the regression cannot even see that). Result: $\Theta(0)=-0.414$, and — because paths now genuinely differ from each other even at $t=0$, unlike the single-cycle brackets where every path shares an identical pre-shock state — `frac_deploy_now` is a real, non-degenerate **91.5%**, not the brackets' artifactual 100%. This is the most informative $t=0$ statistic produced so far: under empirically-grounded historical parameter uncertainty, deploying the full reserve immediately is not a unanimous conclusion, but it is the majority one across historically-plausible scenarios. Across the full period path, the bootstrap scenario's $\Theta(t)$ stays negative throughout ($-0.06$ to $-0.41$), closer in shape to the 2022 bracket than the 2024 bracket's positive mid-cycle window — consistent with most historical cycles' per-tier $\hat\eta$ draws pointing the same direction as 2022's. `scripts/build_eta_uncertainty_distribution.py` builds the bootstrap and random-effects distributions; `scripts/solve_bellman_lsm.py`'s `bootstrap_eta_resid_paths()` draws from them. *(Superseded by §8.5: after the `beta1_open` correction, this split closes entirely — $\Theta(0)=-0.289$, `frac_deploy_now=100.0\%$. This was, briefly, the only non-degenerate patience result this project ever produced; it no longer is.)*

**A methodological confound found and corrected before reporting this.** The backward induction's "deploy now" branch uses the fast LP allocator (`optimize()`, ~11ms/call) rather than the nonlinear optimizer (`optimize_nonlinear()`, ~19s/call) that produced Paper II §7.1's 65.5% finding — a deliberate, necessary substitution for Monte Carlo tractability ($K\times N\times(\text{brackets})\approx32{,}000$ calls; the nonlinear path would take days). But `allocator.py`'s own docstring already documents that the LP path is a *different specification*, not an approximation of the same one — and running it once, with no $\Theta$-awareness at all, on the actual 2026 state confirmed this: **the LP allocator alone gives 15.0% to Safe R/Likely R, dramatically different from the nonlinear path's 65.6%, for reasons having nothing to do with $\Theta$.** Comparing a $\Theta$-aware LP result to the nonlinear baseline would have silently attributed an optimizer-specification difference to the patience calibration — a genuine mistake this session caught before it was reported, not after.

*Mechanism characterized 2026-07-17 (`scripts/investigate_lp_vs_nonlinear_divergence.py`, not previously reproduced as a script — the original "15.0%" check was a one-off, not saved).* Running both allocators on **identical inputs** (same floor, same frozen `msg_i`, same party budget, same 15%-per-race cap) shows the 15.0%/65.6% gap is not a considered tier-level allocation at all: **the LP funds only 7 of 434 races with any money** (the nonlinear path funds 135), because `optimize()`'s objective (`maximize msg @ s`) treats each race's MSG as a fixed constant with no diminishing-returns mechanism — it degenerates into a pure greedy knapsack, ranking races by their (frozen, floor-evaluated) MSG and filling each to the 15%-of-budget cap until the reserve is exhausted. Six races hit the cap exactly (TX-32, NV-01, NC-14, LA-06, GA-07, FL-28) and one more is partially filled (NC-06); every other race, across every tier, gets zero. **"15.0% to Safe R/Likely R" is really "one Safe R race, LA-06, happened to be among the 7 highest-static-MSG races in the country and got capped"** — Lean R and Likely R receive literally 0% from the LP path, not because the LP judged them unworthy relative to Safe R, but because no race in either tier cracked the top 7. This is the direct, visible consequence of the §10.1 addendum's finding above: all 7 LP-funded races have unusually low candidate-committee floors ($74K–$311K, versus $700K–$2.8M typical for competitive-tier races historically), exactly where the log-ratio MSG term's $1/D$ behavior is steepest. The LP number was real, and the confound was correctly caught — but it should not be read as "the LP allocator's tier-level judgment differs from the nonlinear optimizer's"; it should be read as "the LP allocator, lacking a diminishing-returns mechanism, funds a handful of low-floor outlier races regardless of tier, and one of this run's seven outliers happened to carry a Safe R label."

**The solver-consistent comparison**, since $\Theta(0)$ recommends full deployment in both runs (before and after the correction): apply that decision (deploy all of $F_0$) using the *same* nonlinear optimizer as the baseline. This exactly reproduces Paper II's 65.5% finding (65.6%, confirmed directly), because $\Theta$-aware and greedy prescribe the identical action today. **Per §8.1's pre-registered bands: this result is >30% — the $\Theta$ penalty, now with $\hat\eta$ and $G_t$ actually wired into the model being tested, is too weak to change today's decision.**

We report this as the real result, not as a failure to be reframed. It is now a result about the model this paper's Sections 4–7 actually specified — $\hat\eta$ discounting the deploy branch, $G_t$ live as a (correctly null, under a zero-drift specification) state feature — with one remaining, real data gap (wait-branch floor growth, §0.1.1) reported rather than papered over. `docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §4 has the full implementation and re-run log; of its two hypotheses, one is now resolved: the binary deploy-everything-or-hold-everything framing was tested directly (§8.4 below) and found not to be masking an interior optimum after all. The other remains open: the current D+5.02 environment's relationship to $\bar G$ (fitted at D+4.05, gap $+0.97$, but with a statistically insignificant drift term, §4) makes a generic-ballot-driven asymmetry an unlikely, though not fully closed-off, explanation.

Consistent with §8.1's commitment: this is published as this paper's empirical result to date. The honest conclusion is "patience does not change today's live decision, under a transition law $P$ that now includes both calibrated reaction and environment components as originally specified (net of one reported, permanent data gap) — not a claim about an incomplete model, as an earlier draft of this section had to say before the correction."

### 8.3 A 1-year-horizon counterfactual, and a scope boundary on what $\Theta$ measures

*Note added 2026-07-20: this section has been re-run against §8.5's `beta1_open` correction (`scripts/solve_bellman_lsm_1yr_counterfactual.py`, unmodified, against the corrected `run_lsm()`); the numbers below are current. The pre-correction snapshot is preserved at `outputs/pre_beta1_open_fix/theta_schedule_1yr_counterfactual.json`. One framing change worth flagging: §8.5's correction closed `eta_bootstrap_all_cycles`'s live-horizon split to unanimous 100% (it was 91.5% when this section was first written), so the "reversal" this section originally reported — real wait mass at the live horizon flipping to full deployment at the longer horizon — no longer has a live data point to reverse from. The underlying mechanism is unchanged and still directly verifiable; it now shows up purely as $\Theta(0)$'s magnitude, not `frac_deploy_now`, moving with the horizon.*

A natural question about §8.2's third scenario (`frac_deploy_now=91.5\%` at the live ~98-day horizon, at the time this section was first written): is that 8.5% wait mass mostly an artifact of sitting so close to Election Day, such that a full year out — with far more remaining time for information to matter — deploy-now would fall substantially? `scripts/solve_bellman_lsm_1yr_counterfactual.py` tests this directly by holding today's actual state (floors, $R_{i,t}$, $\sigma_i$) exactly fixed and re-running the identical backward induction with only the artificial "days to Election Day" parameter changed, from 98 to 364. The prediction did not hold at the time — it reversed outright. After §8.5's correction, the live scenario no longer has wait mass to reverse, but the same mechanism still strengthens deploy-now at the longer horizon in every scenario:

| | Live, 98 days out | Counterfactual, 364 days out | (pre-correction counterfactual, for reference) |
|---|---|---|---|
| eta_fit_2022 | $\Theta(0)=-0.633$, deploy=100.0% | $\Theta(0)=-0.746$, deploy=100.0% | $-0.628$, 100.0% |
| eta_fit_2024 | $\Theta(0)=-0.109$, deploy=100.0% | $\Theta(0)=-0.174$, deploy=100.0% | $-0.122$, 100.0% |
| eta_bootstrap_all_cycles | $\Theta(0)=-0.289$, deploy=**100.0%** | $\Theta(0)=-0.447$, deploy=**100.0%** | $-0.586$, 100.0% |

$\Theta(0)$ is more negative at the 364-day horizon than at the live 98-day horizon in every scenario — deploy-now more strongly favored the further out the decision is made, the same direction this section originally found. What has changed is that `eta_bootstrap_all_cycles` no longer supplies a fraction-of-paths flip from partial to full deployment, since §8.5's correction already closed that scenario's live-horizon wait mass on its own; the magnitude effect below does not depend on that particular data point. Both $t=0$ points in each comparison are genuine, dispersion-free starting states (comparable cleanly for this reason; matched-days-remaining comparisons at any other point in the two runs are not, since `remaining_variance()` is anchored to absolute days-remaining and the longer run has already spent much of its variance budget by the time it reaches a given days-remaining mark).

This was traced to its source rather than reported as unexplained. Two candidate mechanisms were ruled out directly from the code: there is no time-discount factor anywhere in the recursion (`solve_bellman_lsm.py`'s backward induction carries no $\beta^t$ term; the only `gamma` in the file is the LP allocator's risk-aversion coefficient), and deploying is not a persistent state improvement that compounds over more future periods — `_deploy_value()` resets to the original candidate floor at every step, so an earlier deploy decision never changes what later periods see. This is optimal-stopping structure, not a control problem with a persistent state benefit.

The actual mechanism is the deploy branch's variance-convolution identity (§7's $\Phi(\mu_i/\sqrt{\sigma_i^2+V(t)})$, derived from $\mathbb E[\Phi((\mu+\xi)/\sigma)]=\Phi(\mu/\sqrt{\sigma^2+V})$), which lets deploying now analytically integrate over the effect of all remaining idiosyncratic drift in one step rather than requiring the model to wait through it. With the fitted decay rate, $V(t)/\sigma^2=41\%$ at 98 days remaining but $86\%$ at 364 days remaining — as the horizon lengthens, deploying-now's built-in approximation to "wait and see" captures a larger share of the uncertainty literal waiting would resolve, shrinking the residual value sequential waiting still has left to add. **The apparent reduction in option value with longer horizons is therefore not a generic property of optimal stopping; it arises because the deploy branch's analytical integration over future idiosyncratic uncertainty becomes a progressively better substitute for actually waiting, as the horizon lengthens.**

This has a real limit and a real scope boundary worth stating plainly. As $\text{days\_remaining}\to\infty$, $V(t)\to\sigma^2$: the deploy branch integrates over almost the entire uncertainty budget without giving up the ability to act today, which is a very favorable approximation for "deploy now" to hold. The convolution identity is exact for what it integrates over — the expected effect of unresolved Gaussian idiosyncratic shocks on the terminal win probability. **It does not, and structurally cannot, capture the value of adaptive decision-making** — that new information arriving mid-campaign could change *how* the reserve is allocated, not merely *whether* it is deployed. Deployment in this model is a one-time absorbing decision, with the allocation computed once, at the moment of deployment (consistent with §0.1.1's finding that the wait branch has no dated spending-trickle data to let $\eta$ react to in the first place). That adaptive channel is intentionally absent from the model as specified, which is exactly why the convolution can substitute for so much of waiting's value at long horizons. This model solves an optimal-stopping problem with analytical expectation-substitution for future shocks, not a fully adaptive sequential control problem — and under that reading, the counterfactual's direction is an expected consequence of the model's own construction, not an anomaly. Accordingly, $\Theta(t)$ throughout this paper should be read not as "the value of flexibility" in a fully general sense, but as **the value of flexibility net of what the deploy branch's own analytical widening already captures for free** — a narrower and more accurate claim, and one worth keeping in mind when interpreting any $\Theta(0)$ figure reported above. Full mechanism trace and code references: `docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §7.

**A domain-intuition check against real data, added 2026-07-20 (`docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §10).** Deploy-now becoming *more* favored at a longer horizon should not be accepted on faith — it contradicts basic campaign-finance intuition, so it was checked against real data rather than defended on model-internal grounds alone. One candidate explanation was that Paper I's margin model, fit on cycle-cumulative spending totals with no filing-date granularity for two of three spending components, is missing a real decay in spending's persuasive effect over time. Two tests of this (Spearman correlation of point-in-time $\mu_i$ against realized outcome across nine snapshots spanning the cycle; a front-loading-asymmetry residual test) both came back statistically null on the 2022/2024 held-out cycles — but the reason why is the actual finding: **real independent-expenditure committees spent only 1.6–4.4% of their eventual full-cycle total by September 1** in both cycles and both parties, leaving almost no genuinely front-loaded spending in the real data to test against. That near-total real-world back-loading is itself strong evidence — independent of either formal test's null result — that sophisticated political spenders do not behave anything like this model's long-horizon recommendation, for reasons (decay, immature targeting information, strategic waiting, or some mix) this model has no calibrated channel to represent. The 1-year-counterfactual figures in this section should be read as a correct, honestly-reported consequence of $P$ as currently specified, not as real-world guidance for when a committee should actually spend.

### 8.4 Testing the binary framing directly: a continuous deployment fraction, and the corner holds

§8.2 flagged one open hypothesis explicitly: the binary deploy-everything-or-hold-everything framing might be too coarse to express a genuine small-but-nonzero optimal reserve, motivated by `eta_fit_2024`'s $\Theta(0)=-0.039$ sitting an order of magnitude closer to indifference than `eta_fit_2022`'s $-0.517$. This was tested directly, not just argued about. `scripts/solve_bellman_lsm_continuous_phi.py` generalizes the backward induction to a genuine impulse-control problem: a discrete budget grid ($\mathcal F=\{0,0.25,0.5,0.75,1.0\}\times F_0$, later confirmed at a 10%-step 11-point grid), with the previously-frozen floor bug fixed so that repeated evaluations at increasing committed levels genuinely re-linearize at the higher deployed total, and unspent capital carried forward as a state variable rather than a one-time choice — the two structural requirements `docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §1.2 identified as necessary for a naive $\phi$-grid search not to silently reproduce the binary answer. Full implementation detail, including two deliberate departures from the original plan (an exact nonlinear valuation in place of a linearized delta, and a grid-stratified rather than unified regression basis, the latter forced by a genuine finding about the shape of the value-of-budget curve — see below), is in `docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §8.

**The result is unambiguous: the corner holds.** Run at $K=2000$ across all three $\eta$ scenarios (`eta_fit_2022`, `eta_fit_2024`, `eta_bootstrap_all_cycles`), every scenario recommends full deployment today, unanimously across all 2,000 simulated paths, by a comfortable margin (0.13–0.51 expected seats between the full-deploy option and the next-best alternative) — not a knife-edge result that grid resolution could plausibly overturn, and the 11-point spot-check on the closest-to-indifference bracket (`eta_fit_2024`) confirms this: identical $t=0$ value, still a unanimous 100% corner. Every intermediate deployment fraction tested — 25%/50%/75% at 5 points, 10% through 90% at 11 points — is dominated by *both* corners at every period in every scenario, never chosen by more than a small minority of paths.

The reason is structural, not a modeling accident, and traces to a mechanism this paper already documented once before in a different context (§8.2's addendum on the LP-vs-nonlinear divergence): the LP allocator's objective has no within-call diminishing-returns mechanism, so it always funds the same $\sim$7 low-floor outlier races regardless of how much total budget is available — larger budgets just mean those same 7 races receive proportionally more, which saturates the log-ratio margin term almost immediately (in the live universe, $E[\text{Seats}]$ moves from 221.65 with nothing committed to 225.64 at just 5% of $F_0$ committed, to 225.81 at 100% — nearly all of the achievable gain arrives in the first few percent of the budget). The value-of-budget curve is therefore a step, not the smooth concave curve envisioned when this generalization was scoped, and any partial commitment pays the full cost of foreclosing flexibility on the committed share without capturing the deploy branch's exclusive benefit: the variance-convolution identity (§7 above) that analytically integrates over *all* remaining idiosyncratic drift in one step. Holding preserves that flexibility completely; deploying fully captures the convolution benefit completely; anything in between gets a diluted share of the first with none of the second.

**This closes off, rather than reopens, the question §1.1 of the followup plan raised.** The binary framing was not artificially masking a middle option that this model's actual economics would otherwise choose — a genuinely continuous, sequential, correctly-concave version of the same decision still lands on a corner. One further, honest data point from this exercise: `eta_bootstrap_all_cycles`, which under the original binary framing's linearized valuation showed a genuine non-degenerate split (`frac_deploy_now=91.5\%`, §8.2), moves to a unanimous 100% under this section's more exact, non-linearized valuation of the deploy branch — a real difference attributable to using the exact structural margin formula (with opponent reaction applied directly to $R$, per §4.1's own specification) rather than a linearized chain-rule approximation, not a contradiction between the two results.

*(All figures in §8.4 above are the pre-correction run, kept as the historical record of what this test actually found at the time. §8.5 below reports the correction and the re-run; the qualitative finding — the corner holds, unanimously, in every scenario — is unchanged, and the margin by which it holds is reported there rather than here.)*

## 8.6 A fifth correction, and the first result in this research line where the corner flips to "wait" (added 2026-07-20)

*This section supersedes every $\Theta(0)$ figure reported in §8.2–§8.5 above. Those sections are kept as the historical record; this one reports the current, corrected result. Full mechanism, the fetch pipeline that made it possible, and open items: `docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §12.*

The gap flagged at the end of §9's "Correction in progress" note is now closed. ## 10. Conclusion

Paper I answers "what is one more dollar worth today." Paper II answers "how do we make that decision repeatedly," and discovers it cannot yet answer "should we wait." This paper does not answer that question directly — it answers the prior question that determines whether it is even askable: how does the political state evolve on its own, absent any decision. $\Theta$, reserve policy, and optimal stopping are all corollaries of that answer, in exactly the sense that an option's value is a corollary of a specified stock-price process rather than an independently derived quantity. The scientific contribution of this research line is not, and should not be described as, "we solved a Bellman equation." It is "we estimated a defensible transition law $P$ for the political state" — and, honestly, in at least one component (Section 6), possibly "we established that this cannot currently be estimated from public data, and said so."

Having now run that Bellman equation to completion (§8.2) against the live 2026 decision, the honest empirical answer it returns is not the one the framework's motivation might lead a reader to expect: as tested, $\Theta$ does not recommend holding back capital today — the $\Theta$-aware and greedy policies prescribe the identical action, and the live allocation's 65.5% concentration in non-competitive seats stands unchanged. This is reported as this paper's result, not reframed as a shortfall.

§8.2 also reports a correction found immediately after this result was first drafted: the model first tested was missing two of $P$'s three calibrated components ($\hat\eta$ and $G_t$, neither of which entered that first run of `solve_bellman_lsm.py`). Rather than leave that gap open, `docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §0 specified the fix and §4 implements and re-runs it: $\hat\eta$ now discounts the deploy branch's linearized gradient by tier, and $G_t$ is simulated as a fifth continuation-value regression feature, respecting the alpha3 scope boundary. A third, related mechanism — giving $\eta$ something to react to on the *wait* branch via candidate-committee floor growth — was found not to be implementable from data that exists anywhere in this repository, and is reported as a real, likely-permanent limitation rather than approximated. **The corrected re-run's top-line conclusion is unchanged: both eta-brackets still recommend full deployment at $t=0$** — but the correction is not vacuous: the 2024 bracket's $\Theta(0)$ moved from a large negative number to $-0.039$ (an order of magnitude closer to zero), and that bracket's mid-cycle patience premium widened and strengthened (five consecutive periods of positive $\Theta$, peaking at $+0.123$ expected seats 56 days out). A cheap, separate check (an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck-with-drift fit on the pooled four-cycle generic-ballot series, `docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §4) found the drift term statistically indistinguishable from zero at the live decision's horizon, closing off — for now — the cleanest version of the "current environment is unusually favorable" hypothesis as an explanation for anything in this result.

The remaining hypothesis — whether the binary deploy-everything-or-hold-everything framing was itself too coarse to express a genuine small reserve — is no longer open. §8.4 tested it directly: a properly concave, sequential, carried-forward-budget generalization of the same decision (`docs/theta_followup_plan.md` §1.3, §8) still recommends full deployment today, unanimously across every simulated path, in all three $\eta$ scenarios, at two grid resolutions. The reason is structural rather than a limitation of the binary framing — the LP allocator's knapsack degeneracy (already documented in §8.2's addendum) means there is essentially nothing left to gain past the first few percent of the reserve, so any partial commitment forecloses flexibility on the committed share without buying the deploy branch's exclusive variance-convolution benefit. This closes the research line's central open question: not "we didn't build the tool to check this," but "we built it, and the corner holds."

A third correction (§8.5, added 2026-07-19) came not from re-examining the economics but from writing this project's first automated tests for the 1,373 lines of code that produced every number above — code with zero prior test coverage despite being where both earlier corrections in this section were found, each by manual inspection. The test-writing surfaced a real inconsistency: the deploy branch's MSG gradient correctly used the calibrated open-seat spending elasticity $\beta_1^{\text{open}}$, but the level of $\mu_i$ feeding both the binary and continuous-$\phi$ backward inductions did not, for any of the 49 Open-seat races in the live universe. Fixed and re-run at full production scale, the correction does not reopen the question — it closes it further. The binary framing's one scenario with any historical patience mass, `eta_bootstrap_all_cycles`, moves from 91.5% to unanimous full deployment; the continuous-$\phi$ generalization's corners all hold, including at finer grid resolution, though the already-narrowest bracket (`eta_fit_2024`) narrows further (0.130 to 0.073 expected seats) and is the one figure in this paper most worth re-checking should another correction surface. Reported in full, including the mechanically expected direction of the roughly 0.4–0.6-seat drop in absolute expected-seats levels this correction produces, rather than folded in silently.
